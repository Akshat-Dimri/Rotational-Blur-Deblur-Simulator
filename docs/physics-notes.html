The project involved simulating motion blur resulting from angular motion in electro-optical systems and restoring the degraded imagery using frequency-domain deconvolution techniques in the polar coordinate framework. The methodology included transformation of Cartesian images to polar coordinates, application of asymmetric angular convolution to model directional motion blur, and subsequent deblurring via regularized inverse filtering in the frequency domain. 1. Introduction

1.1 Background & Motivation

In imaging systems subjected to rotational motion — such as satellite platforms, scanning electron microscopes, and surveillance optics mounted on gimbals — images suffer from a specific class of blur known as rotational motion blur. Unlike translational blur, this is spatially variant: the blur extent increases radially outward from the centre of rotation. Such degradation significantly reduces image fidelity, and conventional spatially invariant deblurring methods are ineffective. The motivation of this work is to model and invert the effects of angular blur by leveraging polar domain transformation and a physics-consistent blur model.

1.2 Rotational Motion Blur in Imaging Systems

Angular blur arises when either the imaging sensor or the observed scene undergoes rotational motion during the exposure interval. Two critical hardware components in practical imaging setups contribute:

Mirror-Stabilised Scanning Systems

These systems employ rotating or tilting mirrors to stabilise or scan the field of view. While effective in compensating for certain motions, rapid angular changes during exposure induce rotational blur, especially when the feedback or actuation system has latency or jitter. The image plane receives light along curved trajectories, causing pixel displacements on circular arcs.

Derotation Prisms (Abbe / Pechan)

Used in optical systems to counteract image rotation due to platform motion (e.g. airborne or satellite imaging), derotation prisms rotate to maintain image orientation. Imperfect synchronisation or dynamic rotation during integration time leads to residual angular motion, resulting in spatially variant blur centred at the optical axis.

In both cases, the angular motion trajectory causes each pixel to trace an arc with radius proportional to its distance from the rotation centre, making the blur spatially non-uniform and challenging to reverse with traditional deconvolution algorithms.

1.3 Problem Definition

The goal is to accurately model the rotational blur degradation and formulate effective inversion strategies. The primary challenges include:

  • Designing Cartesian-to-Polar transformations that minimise interpolation loss.
  • Constructing spatially variant blur kernels in polar space.
  • Implementing stable inverse filtering methods that suppress noise amplification and ringing.
  • Evaluating restoration quality quantitatively using PSNR, SSIM, and MSE metrics.

1.4 Project Objectives

  • Implement polar-domain transformations to linearise angular motion blur.
  • Estimate and apply spatially varying angular PSFs across radial coordinates.
  • Develop inverse filtering techniques (Fourier-based and regularized).
  • Evaluate restoration using PSNR, SSIM, and Std. Dev. metrics.
  • Explore extensions to elliptical blur models and non-uniform FOV.

1.5 Overview of Methodology

The method involves transforming the input image from Cartesian to polar coordinates, where angular blur becomes shift-invariant along the angular dimension θ. The image is convolved with an angular PSF that varies with radius. Deblurring is achieved using frequency-domain inverse filtering and regularized deconvolution. The restored image is finally mapped back to the Cartesian domain for visual and quantitative analysis.

Methodology Pipeline

Input image → Cartesian-to-Polar transform (bilinear interpolation) → Angular PSF construction (radius-dependent) → Polar-domain blur simulation → Frequency-domain inverse filtering (regularized) → Polar-to-Cartesian reconversion → Restored image + quality metrics

2. Literature-Derived Theoretical Foundations

2.1 Nature of Rotational Blur and Its Challenges

In image acquisition systems, motion blur arises from relative movement between the camera and the scene during exposure. While linear (translational) motion blur is well-understood and modelled using shift-invariant operations, rotational motion blur introduces spatially varying distortions, making restoration significantly more complex.

Rotational blur is characterised by the fact that each pixel in the image moves along a circular arc centred around a fixed point. The extent of blur increases with distance from this rotation centre — pixels further away experience longer motion paths and more severe blurring. Consequently, the PSF (Point Spread Function) is no longer uniform across the image.

2.2 Shift-Variant vs. Shift-Invariant Approaches

The spatially variant nature of rotational blur means: (i) the blur kernel changes with radius; (ii) a single convolution kernel cannot capture the degradation; and (iii) restoration algorithms must account for position-dependent degradation. Traditional linear deblurring techniques fail because they assume all pixels are blurred identically.

Resolution Strategy

The approach proposed in the literature reformulates the problem using polar coordinates, transforming the variant blur into a more manageable, shift-invariant form along the angular axis. This enables standard linear convolution and frequency-domain inversion per radial slice.

2.3 Polar Domain as a Linearizing Framework

The core idea is to move from the Cartesian image space to a polar coordinate system where the radial component r remains fixed for a given pixel, and the angular component θ captures the motion path. In the polar domain, rotational blur becomes a linear shift along the angular axis, holding the radius constant. This transformation enables the use of standard convolution and inverse filtering techniques in a transformed space.

After the polar transform (right), blur at each fixed radius r becomes a constant-width horizontal smear along θ — enabling standard 1D convolution and FFT-based inversion.

Results Highlights

Reconversion accuracy: Polar-Cartesian transformations preserved original image quality with negligible loss (PSNR > 66 dB, SSIM ≈ 1.0), validating geometric reversibility. Deblurring performance: Inverse filtering restored up to ~12 dB PSNR over blurred inputs; performance was superior for synthetic point-like targets and weaker on complex textured scenes. Boundary handling: Wraparound in polar domain minimised angular discontinuities and enabled stable deconvolution, though high-frequency ringing remained near radial extremes.

Experimental Filters (Not in Final Pipeline)

Bilateral filtering provided edge-aware smoothing but added halo artefacts in thin lines. Median filtering was effective for impulsive noise but flattened fine features. Unsharp masking increased local contrast but introduced overshoots near edges. Total Variation denoising flattened low-frequency areas too aggressively. Wiener filtering in the polar domain was difficult to calibrate due to unknown noise power spectrum.

11. Conclusion

This project focused on the restoration of images affected by directional angular motion blur induced by rotational imaging systems using a polar-domain modelling approach combined with inverse filtering. The spatially variant nature of rotational blur was addressed by transforming the image from Cartesian to polar coordinates, wherein the blur becomes approximately shift-invariant along the angular axis.

Key Contributions

Development of a Python-based, modular pipeline to simulate rotational blur using radius-dependent angular convolution kernels and restore degraded images via FFT-based inverse filtering · Design of a coordinate transformation framework preserving spatial and intensity fidelity · Construction of spatially variant blur kernels for uniform and Gaussian angular profiles · Implementation of regularized inverse filtering to suppress noise amplification · Comprehensive evaluation using PSNR, SSIM, MSE, Std Dev., and visual validation.

The proposed method effectively mitigated rotational blur across a wide range of test cases. The system showed flexibility across image types and robustness in handling varying angular velocities and exposure settings. This work lays a scalable foundation for advanced restoration models capable of addressing real-world complexities such as blind kernel estimation, elliptical blur, and variable FOV conditions, with direct applicability in surveillance, medical, astronomical, and remote sensing imaging domains.
